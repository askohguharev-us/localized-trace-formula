\chapter*{Executive summary}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Executive summary}
\markboth{Executive summary}{Executive summary}

\noindent
The Selberg trace formula is a fundamental identity relating the spectral theory of the Laplace--Beltrami operator on a hyperbolic surface to the geometry of closed geodesics.
In its classical global form, it expresses a weighted trace of the wave group (or of a suitable functional calculus of the Laplacian) as a sum over the conjugacy classes of the underlying Fuchsian group.
The present monograph develops a systematic \emph{localized} framework for the trace formula, designed to isolate the contributions of specific spectral windows and specific geometric regimes, while retaining a precise control of the remainder terms generated by truncation, smoothing, and microlocalization.

\medskip

\noindent
The central objective is to construct a robust analytic formalism in which the trace formula becomes an instrument of \emph{local} spectral analysis.
Rather than treating the trace identity as a single global equality, we reinterpret it as a family of quantitative identities indexed by test profiles and localization parameters.
This approach yields a refined mechanism for separating the identity term, the hyperbolic term (closed geodesics), and, in the noncompact finite-area case, the parabolic and continuous-spectrum contributions.
The resulting theory is designed to be compatible with modern microlocal analysis and semiclassical techniques, thereby allowing local trace identities to be inserted into broader analytic arguments.

\section*{Main contributions}

\subsection*{(1) A canonical localized trace functional}

We introduce a localized trace functional $\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi]$ defined for admissible test functions $h$ and auxiliary cutoffs $\chi$.
The functional is constructed by subtracting a controlled low-frequency normalization and is shown to admit a decomposition of the form
\[
\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
=
\mathcal{I}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
+
\mathcal{H}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
+
\mathcal{C}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
+
\mathcal{E}_\Gamma[h;\chi],
\]
where $\mathcal{I}_\Gamma$ is the identity contribution, $\mathcal{H}_\Gamma$ is the hyperbolic contribution (sum over closed geodesics), $\mathcal{C}_\Gamma$ denotes the continuous-spectrum component (absent in the compact case), and $\mathcal{E}_\Gamma$ is a remainder ledger.
The defining feature is that each term in this decomposition is stable under admissible refinement of the localization scheme and admits explicit quantitative bounds.

\subsection*{(2) A ledger calculus for localization errors}

A persistent difficulty in localized trace methods is the accumulation of errors coming from smoothing, cutoff insertion, and microlocal partitioning.
We formalize these contributions through a ledger structure, which behaves additively and is stable under concatenation of estimates.
This provides a canonical way to propagate error bounds across multi-step constructions without losing control of the dependence on the localization parameters.

In particular, we isolate the principal mechanisms producing error terms:
\begin{itemize}
  \item truncation and tail decay of test functions;
  \item quantization ambiguities in pseudodifferential localization;
  \item refinement instabilities under phase-space subdivision;
  \item continuous-spectrum boundary contributions (in the cofinite case).
\end{itemize}
The resulting bookkeeping system is designed to be usable in subsequent applications where multiple local trace identities must be combined.

\subsection*{(3) Compatibility with microlocal and semiclassical analysis}

We place the localized trace identity in a framework compatible with semiclassical scaling.
This permits the insertion of microlocal projectors $\Pi_j$ and their refinements $\widetilde{\Pi}_k$ directly into the trace formula.
The operator-level partition yields local trace contributions of the form
\[
\Tr\bigl(\Pi_j\, h(\sqrt{\Delta-\tfrac14})\, \Pi_j\bigr),
\]
which can be compared across different partitions.
A key point is that the resulting trace identities remain stable under refinement, with the ledger capturing the exact cost of increased microlocal resolution.

\subsection*{(4) A uniform treatment of compact and finite-area settings}

While the compact case is structurally simpler, applications frequently require finite-area surfaces where the Laplacian has continuous spectrum.
We develop the localized trace formula in a way that does not collapse in the presence of scattering.
In particular, we express the continuous-spectrum contribution through explicit scattering terms and show that it can be incorporated into the ledger framework without ambiguity.
This yields a unified trace mechanism valid for both cocompact and cofinite Fuchsian groups.

\section*{Structure of the monograph}

The monograph is organized into a sequence of chapters that progressively build the localized framework.
After fixing geometric conventions and spectral normalizations, we review the classical Selberg trace formula and its analytic prerequisites.
We then develop the localization procedure in two stages:
first, a functional-analytic localization based on admissible test functions and frequency cutoffs;
second, a microlocal localization based on pseudodifferential partitions and semiclassical refinement.
The later chapters establish stability results and provide explicit bounds for all ledger components.

The final chapters discuss how the localized trace formula can be used as a modular component in broader analytic programs, including refined spectral counting, resonance localization, and applications to trace identities of arithmetic origin.

\section*{Guiding philosophy and scope}

The guiding principle of the present work is that the Selberg trace formula should be treated as a \emph{local analytic instrument} rather than a single global equality.
The localized formulation developed here is intended to serve as a stable interface between hyperbolic geometry and spectral analysis, in which one can isolate specific spectral windows, control the contribution of geometric classes, and maintain a uniform error calculus.

This monograph does not attempt to survey all applications of the trace formula.
Instead, it aims to provide a rigorous, reusable framework that can be inserted into future investigations, including problems where local spectral structure must be compared across scales or across families of hyperbolic surfaces.
