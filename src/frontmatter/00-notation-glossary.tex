\section*{General conventions}

\paragraph{Sets, indices, and asymptotics.}
We write $\N=\{1,2,3,\dots\}$ and $\R$ for the real line.
The letter $C$ denotes a positive constant whose value may change from line to line.
For quantities $A,B\ge 0$, the notation $A\ll B$ means $A\le CB$ for a constant $C$ depending only on the fixed geometric and analytic data of the setup; we write $A\ll_{\alpha,\beta} B$ when $C$ is allowed to depend additionally on parameters $\alpha,\beta$.
We use $A\asymp B$ to indicate $A\ll B$ and $B\ll A$.

\paragraph{Fourier transform.}
For $f\in L^1(\R)$ we use the convention
\[
\widehat f(t)\;=\;\int_{\R} f(x)\,e^{-itx}\,dx,
\qquad
f(x)\;=\;\frac1{2\pi}\int_{\R}\widehat f(t)\,e^{itx}\,dt,
\]
whenever the inversion formula applies. (All transforms in this monograph are with respect to this convention.)

\paragraph{Even/odd parts.}
For a function $h:\R\to\C$ we set
\[
h^{\mathrm{ev}}(t)=\frac12\bigl(h(t)+h(-t)\bigr),
\qquad
h^{\mathrm{odd}}(t)=\frac12\bigl(h(t)-h(-t)\bigr),
\]
so that $h=h^{\mathrm{ev}}+h^{\mathrm{odd}}$.

\section*{Geometry and spectral data}

\paragraph{Hyperbolic surface and Laplacian.}
Throughout, $X=\Gamma\backslash\H$ denotes a hyperbolic surface of finite area (compact or cofinite, as specified in the main assumptions).
We write $\Delta$ for the positive Laplace--Beltrami operator on $X$.
In the compact case, the spectrum of $\Delta$ is discrete:
\[
0=\lambda_0<\lambda_1\le\lambda_2\le\dots,\qquad \lambda_j\to+\infty.
\]
It is convenient to parameterize eigenvalues as
\[
\lambda_j=\frac14+r_j^2,\qquad r_j\in \R_{\ge 0}\ \text{ or }\ r_j\in i(0,\tfrac12],
\]
with the usual convention that exceptional eigenvalues correspond to purely imaginary $r_j$.

\paragraph{Spectral counting.}
For $R\ge 0$ we write
\[
N_X(R)=\#\{j:\ 0\le r_j\le R\},
\]
counted with multiplicity. When continuous spectrum is present (cofinite case), spectral measures and scattering data are introduced in the relevant chapter; the present glossary records only notation that is used globally.

\paragraph{Closed geodesics and conjugacy classes.}
Hyperbolic conjugacy classes $[\gamma]$ in $\Gamma$ correspond to primitive closed geodesics on $X$.
We denote by $\ell(\gamma)>0$ the length of the associated closed geodesic, and by $\gamma_0$ the primitive element underlying $\gamma=\gamma_0^k$.
The centralizer order and standard weights are as in the Selberg trace formula conventions used in this monograph.

\section*{Test functions and functional calculus}

\paragraph{Test class.}
The trace formula is formulated for a class of admissible test functions $h:\R\to\C$.
Depending on the global setting, $h$ is assumed to be Schwartz, or to belong to a Paley--Wiener class with prescribed exponential type.
The precise admissibility axioms are stated in the chapters where the trace formula is proved and localized.

\paragraph{Functional calculus.}
Given an admissible $h$, we write $h(\sqrt{\Delta-\tfrac14})$ for the operator defined by the spectral theorem (or by the corresponding functional calculus in the presence of continuous spectrum).
The trace formula expresses $\Tr\,h(\sqrt{\Delta-\tfrac14})$ (or its localized analogue) as a sum of geometric terms.

\paragraph{Cutoff profiles and normalization.}
We fix smooth cutoff profiles $\chi\in C_c^\infty(\R)$ satisfying $\chi\equiv 1$ near $0$.
Given $h$, we define the normalized test
\[
h_\chi \;=\; h - h(0)\chi.
\]
This normalization eliminates the purely local ``zero-mode'' contribution and isolates the geometrically meaningful part of the trace at the level of precision needed later.

\section*{Localized trace functionals and ledgers}

\paragraph{Raw localized trace functional.}
We denote by $\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h]$ the (raw) localized trace functional built from the fixed operator/projector setup.
It decomposes into an identity contribution, contributions from hyperbolic conjugacy classes, and (when applicable) continuous spectrum terms.

\paragraph{Canonical normalized trace.}
For an admissible pair $(h,\chi)$ we write
\[
\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi] \;:=\;\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h_\chi]
\;=\;\mathcal{L}_\Gamma\bigl[h-h(0)\chi\bigr].
\]
The dependence on auxiliary choices is quantified through explicit correction terms and error ledgers.

\paragraph{Error ledgers.}
Throughout the text we track remainder terms by \emph{ledgers} that behave additively under concatenation of estimates.
A typical ledger has the form
\[
\mathcal{E}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
=\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{quant}}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
+\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{ref}}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
+\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{tail}}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
+\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{cont}}_\Gamma[h;\chi],
\]
encoding, respectively, quantization dependence, microlocal refinement dependence, tail truncation errors, and continuous spectrum contributions.
The precise definitions and bounds for these terms are given where they are first used.

\section*{Operator and microlocal notation}

\paragraph{Pseudodifferential operators.}
We write $\Op(a)$ for a quantization of a symbol $a$, with the convention (Kohn--Nirenberg, Weyl, or semiclassical) fixed in the microlocal analysis chapter.
Symbol classes, seminorms, and remainder bounds follow the standard conventions in semiclassical/microlocal analysis.

\paragraph{Partitions of unity and projectors.}
Microlocal localizations are implemented via a finite family $\{\Pi_j\}$ of projectors or cutoff operators forming a partition at the operator level, as specified in the localization chapter.
A refinement $\{\widetilde{\Pi}_k\}$ denotes an admissible microlocal cover that resolves the previous partition at smaller phase-space scales.

\section*{Bibliographic shorthand}

When referring to standard sources for background material, we use the citations in the bibliography; in particular,
Selberg trace and hyperbolic spectral theory references include \cite{Hejhal1983,Buser1992,Iwaniec2002},
while microlocal and semiclassical conventions follow \cite{Zworski2012,DyatlovZworski2019,Sogge2014}.
