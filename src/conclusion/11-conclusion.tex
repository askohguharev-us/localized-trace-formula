\chapter{Conclusion and outlook}
\label{chap:11-conclusion}

\section{Summary of the main contributions}
\label{sec:11.summary}

The aim of this work has been to construct a localized trace framework in which the trace formula is treated not as a
single global identity but as a \emph{stable object} admitting canonicalization under the admissible modifications that
inevitably arise in microlocal analysis: changes of cutoffs, quantization schemes, and partitions of unity.

The main achievement is the introduction of a normalization layer that converts the raw localized trace into a
canonical functional together with a quantitative error ledger.  This yields a robust trace formalism in which
subsequent spectral consequences become invariant statements rather than artifacts of auxiliary choices.

More precisely, the central outputs of the thesis may be summarized as follows.

\begin{enumerate}
\item A systematic construction of localized trace functionals associated with a fixed geometric input and a fixed
operator-theoretic setup, incorporating microlocal projectors and admissible test families.

\item A normalization procedure removing the zero-mode ambiguity (identity-sector instability) by subtraction of a
compactly supported reference profile.

\item A stability theorem asserting that the normalized trace is canonical modulo an explicitly computable identity
correction and a controlled error term.

\item A consolidated error ledger that packages all secondary sources of non-canonicity
(quantization remainders, refinement errors, truncation tails, and continuous-spectrum contributions)
into a single quantified object.

\item A trace-to-zeta bridge: a stable analytic interface connecting the normalized trace formalism to determinant-level
quantities, explicit formulae, and positivity criteria.
\end{enumerate}

Taken together, these results provide a structural framework in which localized trace identities can be used as
reliable analytic tools in spectral geometry and automorphic analysis.

\section{Normalization as canonicalization of trace objects}
\label{sec:11.normalization}

The normalization mechanism introduced in Chapter~\ref{chap:08-normalization} is conceptually simple but technically
decisive.  The raw trace functional $\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h]$ contains a built-in instability at the identity sector:
changing the cutoff profile modifies the contribution of the ``zero mode'' $h(0)$, and this modification propagates
into later statements unless explicitly removed.

To eliminate this ambiguity, one fixes a reference profile $\chi\in C_c^\infty(\R)$ with $\chi\equiv 1$ near $0$ and
introduces the normalization map
\[
\mathsf{N}_\chi(h)=h-h(0)\chi.
\]
The normalized trace is then defined by
\[
\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
:=
\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[\mathsf{N}_\chi(h)].
\]

The fundamental point is that $\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi]$ depends on $\chi$ only through an explicitly computable
identity-term correction.  In particular, for any two admissible cutoffs $\chi_1,\chi_2$ one has the exact identity
\[
\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi_1]-\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi_2]
=
-h(0)\,\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[\chi_1-\chi_2],
\]
and the right-hand side can be evaluated explicitly once the identity contribution is known.

This establishes the principle that the normalized trace functional is canonical modulo a deterministic correction and
thus can be treated as an invariant object rather than a formal expression.

\section{Stability under quantization and microlocal refinement}
\label{sec:11.quantization}

A second structural obstruction to canonicity arises from the microlocal implementation itself.
Different quantization schemes (e.g.\ Weyl versus Kohn--Nirenberg) and different microlocal partitions lead to
formally different kernel representations and hence to formally different trace expansions.
However, such differences are expected to affect only lower-order terms and must therefore be absorbed into the
remainder calculus.

The framework developed in Chapter~\ref{chap:08-normalization} isolates this issue by introducing error ledgers
\[
\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{quant}}_\Gamma[h;\chi],
\qquad
\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{ref}}_\Gamma[h;\chi],
\]
measuring the discrepancy induced by changing quantization or refining the microlocal cover.
The key output is that these discrepancies are \emph{quantified} and are of lower order in the relevant asymptotic
regime.

This leads to the normalization stability theorem:
for any two admissible choices $(\chi,\Op,\Pi)$ and $(\chi',\Op',\Pi')$ one has
\[
\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi]-\mathcal{T}'_\Gamma[h;\chi']
=
\Delta_{\mathrm{id}}(h;\chi,\chi')
+
\Err_{\mathrm{norm}}(h),
\]
where $\Delta_{\mathrm{id}}$ is explicit and
\[
|\Err_{\mathrm{norm}}(h)|
\le
\mathcal{E}_\Gamma[h;\chi]+\mathcal{E}'_\Gamma[h;\chi'].
\]

This result converts the microlocal trace formula from a family of formally distinct expansions into a single
canonical object equipped with a quantitative stability guarantee.

\section{The consolidated error ledger as a structural invariant}
\label{sec:11.ledger}

A notable conceptual output of this work is the systematic use of an explicit error ledger.
Rather than treating remainders as an informal part of the argument, the ledger is constructed as a single
quantitative object
\[
\mathcal{E}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
=
\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{quant}}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
+
\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{ref}}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
+
\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{tail}}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
+
\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{cont}}_\Gamma[h;\chi].
\]

The importance of this consolidation is twofold.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{First}, it isolates the noncanonical content of the trace formula into a single controlled term.
This allows one to formulate later results in an invariant way without repeatedly revisiting the auxiliary
choices of the microlocal setup.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Second}, it provides a natural interface with applications.
In semiclassical or large-parameter regimes, the effectiveness of trace methods depends on the scale separation
between the main term and the remainder.
The ledger provides the exact object that must be minimized or optimized in order to obtain meaningful spectral
consequences.

In this sense, the ledger should be viewed not merely as a technical tool but as an analytic invariant associated
with the localization mechanism itself.

\section{From normalized traces to zeta-type objects}
\label{sec:11.zeta}

Chapter~\ref{chap:10-trace-to-zeta} records the standard analytic interfaces connecting trace formulae to zeta-type
functions, in a form compatible with the normalized trace framework.

The key point is that the normalized trace functional $\mathcal{T}_\Gamma$ may be used as a stable encoder of spectral
information through smoothed oscillatory test families.  A typical family is
\[
h_{\tau,\Delta}(t)=W(t/\Delta)e^{-i\tau t},
\]
where $W\in C_c^\infty(\R)$ is even.
The resulting observable
\[
\mathfrak{D}(\tau;\Delta):=\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h_{\tau,\Delta}]
\]
is stable under admissible modifications and admits an explicit decomposition into geometric and spectral terms with a
controlled error.

In determinant-level language, trace identities allow the reconstruction of zeta-regularized determinants, and in
settings admitting an Euler product one obtains logarithmic derivatives of zeta-type functions from geometric trace
data.

Finally, the positivity mechanism is encoded by convolution squares:
if $h^\ast(t)=\overline{h(-t)}$ and $h\star h^\ast$ is defined by convolution, then
\[
\widehat{h\star h^\ast}(\xi)=|\widehat{h}(\xi)|^2\ge 0,
\]
and thus
\[
\mathcal{Q}[h]:=\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h\star h^\ast]
\]
naturally yields a quadratic form whose sign constraints spectral parameter distributions.
This is the canonical analytic route underlying Li--Weil type positivity criteria.

The conceptual outcome is that the normalized trace formalism provides a robust bridge from microlocal trace analysis
to determinant identities and zeta-type analytic constraints, with all noncanonical dependence confined to explicit
corrections and a controlled remainder.

\section{Interpretation and methodological significance}
\label{sec:11.methodology}

Beyond its technical statements, the present work proposes a methodological viewpoint:
\emph{trace formulae should be treated as canonical objects, not as formal expansions.}

In classical treatments, one often writes a trace formula as an equality between a spectral side and a geometric side,
with auxiliary choices hidden in the construction.
The resulting formula is correct, but its use in later arguments may become fragile unless invariance under these
choices is explicitly tracked.

The normalization layer introduced here provides a systematic remedy.
It is not merely a cosmetic modification: it is a structural upgrade that transforms the trace formula into a stable
analytic object with a well-defined error budget.
This stability is precisely what is required if trace methods are to be used as foundational tools in problems where
the margin between the main term and the error term is critical.

\section{Limitations and open analytic seams}
\label{sec:11.open}

The framework developed in this thesis is designed to be portable across a range of trace-formula settings.
However, several analytic seams remain that require further work in specific applications.

\subsection{Sharp remainder bounds}
\label{subsec:11.open.remainder}

The consolidated ledger $\mathcal{E}_\Gamma[h;\chi]$ provides a canonical packaging of errors, but its effectiveness
depends on sharp estimates.
In particular, in regimes where one aims to extract fine spectral statistics or to establish positivity inequalities
with small margins, one requires remainder bounds beyond the level needed for qualitative trace identities.

A principal direction for future work is the systematic optimization of the ledger in scale-dependent regimes, with
special attention to the interaction between cutoff width, microlocal resolution, and semiclassical scaling.

\subsection{Continuous spectrum and scattering determinants}
\label{subsec:11.open.cont}

In noncompact settings, the continuous spectrum contributes scattering terms that must be incorporated into the
normalization procedure.
While the present framework includes a continuous-spectrum ledger term $\mathcal{E}^{\mathrm{cont}}_\Gamma[h;\chi]$,
the explicit computation and minimization of this term depends on detailed information about the scattering matrix
and its analytic continuation.

A refined analysis of these terms, in particular their behavior under microlocal refinement and under changes of test
profiles, is essential for determinant-level applications.

\subsection{Determinant-level completion}
\label{subsec:11.open.det}

Although Chapter~\ref{chap:10-trace-to-zeta} provides the abstract determinant interface, a full implementation in a
given geometric setting requires the construction of the appropriate operator family $K(s)$ whose determinant yields
the zeta function.
In Selberg-type settings this is classical, but in more general microlocal contexts the operator-theoretic input may
be subtle.

A natural goal is to develop a systematic dictionary between localized trace functionals and Fredholm determinants in
settings beyond constant curvature, including variable curvature or dynamical zeta frameworks.

\section{Final conclusion}
\label{sec:11.final}

The principal outcome of this work is the construction of a normalization framework that upgrades localized trace
formulae into stable analytic objects.
The normalized trace functional $\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi]$ is canonical modulo an explicit identity correction, and
its dependence on auxiliary microlocal choices is quantitatively controlled by a consolidated error ledger.

This yields a robust trace calculus in which subsequent spectral consequences---local Weyl laws, oscillatory trace
observables, determinant identities, explicit formulae, and positivity criteria---may be formulated invariantly and
applied without ambiguity.

The framework therefore provides a foundation for further investigations in spectral geometry and automorphic analysis,
particularly in regimes where stability and sharp error control are decisive.
