\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chap:01-introduction}

\section{Background and motivation}
\label{sec:01.background}

Trace formulae form a foundational tool in spectral geometry, analytic number theory, and microlocal analysis.
In their classical realizations---most notably the Selberg trace formula for hyperbolic surfaces and the Arthur trace
formula for arithmetic quotients---they provide an exact identity relating the spectrum of a natural self-adjoint
operator to geometric and dynamical invariants of the underlying space.

From a geometric perspective, trace identities encode a correspondence between eigenvalue distributions and the
periodic orbit structure of the geodesic flow.  From an analytic perspective, they serve as a mechanism for
converting spectral information into oscillatory integrals and exponential sums.  From an arithmetic perspective,
they connect automorphic representations with zeta-type objects and explicit formulae.

In a substantial range of applications, however, one does not employ the trace formula in a purely global form.
Instead, one introduces cutoffs, partitions of unity, and phase-space localization in order to isolate spectral
information at a prescribed scale.  Such microlocal regimes occur naturally in semiclassical spectral asymptotics,
in scattering theory for noncompact quotients, and in determinant-level applications where one needs to control
regularizations with quantitative precision.

The analytic cost of localization is the introduction of auxiliary choices: cutoff profiles, quantization schemes,
and microlocal decompositions.  While the final spectral statements should be invariant under these choices,
intermediate localized trace identities typically are not.  This dependence is often harmless for coarse asymptotics,
but it becomes decisive in regimes where stability is required at the level of refined remainders, positivity
criteria, or determinant identities.

The purpose of this monograph is to develop a \emph{canonical localized trace formalism} in which the trace identity
is treated not merely as an equality of distributions, but as a stable analytic object equipped with a quantitative
error budget.

\section{The stability problem for localized traces}
\label{sec:01.problem}

Let $M$ be a finite-area hyperbolic surface (possibly with cusps) and let $\Delta$ denote the Laplace--Beltrami
operator on $M$ acting on $L^2(M)$.  Denote by $\Gamma$ the geometric input, so that $M=\Gamma\backslash\mathbb{H}$.
A trace formula is naturally encoded by a distributional functional
\[
\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h],
\]
defined on a suitable class of test functions $h:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{C}$.
In the Selberg framework, $\mathcal{L}_\Gamma$ decomposes into an identity contribution, a sum over nontrivial
conjugacy classes, and in the noncompact case additional continuous-spectrum terms expressed through the scattering
matrix.

In microlocal settings, $\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h]$ is realized as the trace of a localized operator obtained from a
functional calculus of $\Delta$, combined with phase-space projectors and quantization of symbols.
Such a representation is not unique.  Different admissible cutoff profiles and different quantization conventions
produce formally distinct kernel expansions, even when they encode the same underlying spectral information.

A principal source of instability is the identity sector.  Changing the cutoff profile modifies the contribution of
$h(0)$ and hence produces deterministic but nontrivial perturbations of the localized trace.  Further discrepancies
arise from the lower-order sensitivity of pseudodifferential quantization and from refinements of microlocal covers.
If these effects are not explicitly isolated, one risks confusing artifacts of auxiliary choices with genuine
spectral information.

The fundamental question addressed in this work is therefore the following:

\begin{quote}
\emph{Can one construct a canonical localized trace functional whose dependence on auxiliary microlocal data is
explicitly quantified and confined to a controlled remainder?}
\end{quote}

The main results provide an affirmative answer by introducing a normalization mechanism that removes the identity
instability and by assembling all remaining non-canonicity into an explicit error ledger.

\section{Outline of the approach}
\label{sec:01.approach}

The construction developed in this monograph is based on three structural components.

\subsection{Microlocal localization}
\label{subsec:01.approach.localization}

We work with a microlocal partition of unity in phase space, implemented by a family of operators
$\{\Pi_j\}_{j\in J}$ that localize to a controlled cover of the cotangent bundle.
Given an admissible test function $h$, we consider the corresponding functional calculus operator $K_h=h(\sqrt{\Delta})$
(or an equivalent spectral kernel).
This yields a localized trace representation of the form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:01.microlocal-trace}
\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h]
=
\sum_{j\in J}\Tr\bigl(\Pi_j\,K_h\,\Pi_j\bigr)
+
\Err(h),
\end{equation}
where the remainder $\Err(h)$ is controlled by standard microlocal calculus and depends on the localization scale.

\subsection{Canonical normalization of the identity sector}
\label{subsec:01.approach.normalization}

To remove the zero-mode instability, we fix a cutoff profile $\chi\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb{R})$ satisfying
$\chi\equiv 1$ in a neighborhood of $0$ and define the normalization operator
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:01.normalization-map}
\mathsf{N}_\chi(h)
:=
h-h(0)\chi.
\end{equation}
The associated normalized trace functional is defined by
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:01.normalized-trace}
\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
:=
\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[\mathsf{N}_\chi(h)].
\end{equation}
The subtraction in \eqref{eq:01.normalization-map} removes the identity ambiguity and isolates the nontrivial
geometric and dynamical content of the trace.

\subsection{Quantitative stability and error accounting}
\label{subsec:01.approach.ledger}

The remaining dependence on microlocal data is captured by an explicit error ledger.
The ledger is constructed as a sum of terms controlling changes of quantization, refinement of the microlocal cover,
truncation tails, and in the noncompact case contributions of the continuous spectrum.
The central stability theorem shows that all admissible constructions lead to the same normalized trace functional,
up to an explicit deterministic identity correction and a quantitatively controlled remainder.

This organization has two advantages.
First, it yields a canonical trace object that may be used reliably across different microlocal implementations.
Second, it provides a direct analytic interface for applications where the effectiveness of trace methods depends on
sharp remainder control.

\section{Statement of the main results}
\label{sec:01.main-results}

We now summarize the principal conclusions of this work in a simplified form.
Precise hypotheses and quantitative versions are given in the corresponding chapters.

\begin{theorem}[Canonical normalized trace functional]
\label{thm:01.canonical-trace}
Let $\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h]$ denote the trace functional associated with the geometric input $\Gamma$.
Fix $\chi\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb{R})$ with $\chi\equiv 1$ near $0$, and define
\[
\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
=
\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h-h(0)\chi].
\]
Then the functional $\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi]$ is canonical modulo an explicit identity-sector correction depending
only on $h(0)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Cutoff change identity]
\label{thm:01.cutoff-change}
Let $\chi_1,\chi_2\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb{R})$ satisfy $\chi_j\equiv 1$ in a neighborhood of $0$.
Then for any admissible test function $h$ one has the exact relation
\[
\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi_1]-\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi_2]
=
-h(0)\,\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[\chi_1-\chi_2].
\]
In particular, the cutoff dependence of the normalized trace is confined to a deterministic correction supported in
the identity sector.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Stability under admissible microlocal modifications]
\label{thm:01.stability}
Let $\Op$ and $\Op'$ be two admissible quantization schemes and let $\{\Pi_j\}$ and $\{\widetilde{\Pi}_k\}$ be two
admissible microlocal covers.
Then for each admissible test function $h$ there exists a remainder term $\Err_{\mathrm{norm}}(h)$ such that
\[
\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi]-\mathcal{T}'_\Gamma[h;\chi']
=
\Delta_{\mathrm{id}}(h;\chi,\chi')+\Err_{\mathrm{norm}}(h),
\]
where $\Delta_{\mathrm{id}}(h;\chi,\chi')$ is an explicit identity correction and
\[
|\Err_{\mathrm{norm}}(h)|
\le
\mathcal{E}_\Gamma[h;\chi]+\mathcal{E}'_\Gamma[h;\chi'].
\]
Here $\mathcal{E}_\Gamma$ and $\mathcal{E}'_\Gamma$ are explicit error ledgers arising from the microlocal calculus.
\end{theorem}

Theorems~\ref{thm:01.canonical-trace}--\ref{thm:01.stability} provide a structural upgrade of localized trace methods:
instead of working with a family of trace identities depending on auxiliary choices, one obtains a canonical trace
functional equipped with a quantitative stability guarantee.

\section{Consequences and applications}
\label{sec:01.applications}

The canonicalization mechanism has two immediate consequences.

\subsection{Local spectral asymptotics}
\label{subsec:01.applications.weyl}

The normalized trace functional provides a robust framework for deriving local Weyl laws and microlocal spectral
asymptotics.  In particular, one can isolate the principal Weyl term and control the remainder uniformly under
refinements of the localization scale and admissible changes of auxiliary microlocal data.

\subsection{Determinant-level trace-to-zeta interfaces}
\label{subsec:01.applications.zeta}

Trace formulae are naturally connected to zeta-regularized determinants and explicit formulae.
The normalized trace functional yields a stable interface for passing from localized trace expansions to
determinant identities and positivity mechanisms, since all noncanonical dependence is isolated into explicit
identity corrections and a quantified remainder ledger.

This provides a canonical route from microlocal trace analysis to zeta-type constraints, including Li--Weil type
positivity criteria in settings where such criteria are available.

\section{Organization of the monograph}
\label{sec:01.organization}

The monograph is organized as follows.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:02-preliminaries}.}
We introduce the geometric framework, the relevant operator calculus, and the admissible test classes.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chapters~\ref{chap:03-kernel}--\ref{chap:05-microlocal}.}
We construct the localized kernels and microlocal projectors and develop the quantitative estimates required for trace
localization.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:06-geometric}.}
We establish the localized trace formula and identify the decomposition into identity and nontrivial geometric terms.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:08-normalization}.}
We introduce the normalization layer and prove stability under admissible microlocal modifications.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:09-local-weyl}.}
We derive local Weyl asymptotics in terms of the canonical normalized trace functional and its error ledger.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:10-trace-to-zeta}.}
We develop trace-to-zeta interfaces and discuss determinant-level consequences and positivity mechanisms.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:11-conclusion}.}
We summarize the results and indicate directions for further development.
