\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection{Background and motivation}
\label{subsec:intro.background}

Trace formulae occupy a central position in spectral geometry, analytic number theory, and microlocal analysis.
In their classical incarnations---such as the Selberg trace formula for compact hyperbolic surfaces or the Arthur trace
formula for arithmetic quotients---they provide a precise identity relating spectral data of a natural operator
(Laplace--Beltrami, Casimir, or a representation-theoretic analogue) to geometric and dynamical invariants
encoded by closed geodesics and conjugacy classes.

From the analytic viewpoint, trace identities constitute a mechanism for translating spectral questions into
oscillatory integrals and exponential sums.  From the geometric viewpoint, they encode a deep correspondence between
the distribution of eigenvalues and the periodic orbit structure of the underlying geodesic flow.
From the arithmetic viewpoint, they serve as a bridge between automorphic representations and zeta-type objects.

In many applications, however, one does not use the trace formula in its global form.
Instead, one introduces cutoffs and partitions of unity, localizes the kernel in phase space, and extracts
asymptotics from a \emph{microlocal trace} at a prescribed scale.
Such localization procedures are ubiquitous: they appear in semiclassical spectral asymptotics, in scattering theory
for noncompact quotients, and in the analysis of dynamical zeta functions.

The price of localization is that one must choose auxiliary objects: cutoff profiles, quantization conventions,
and microlocal covers.  While the final spectral statements should not depend on these choices, the intermediate
trace identities typically do.  This dependence is often suppressed in the literature, as it is harmless for
coarse asymptotics; yet it becomes critical in any regime where one aims for stability at the level of
determinant identities, positivity criteria, or fine remainder bounds.

The purpose of this work is to develop a \emph{canonical localized trace formalism} in which the trace identity is
promoted from a formula to a stable analytic object.  The central goal is to construct a normalized trace functional
together with an explicit remainder ledger, in such a way that all subsequent applications depend only on
invariants that are stable under admissible changes of auxiliary data.

\subsection{The main problem: stability of localized trace objects}
\label{subsec:intro.problem}

Let $X$ be a geometric space equipped with a natural Laplace-type operator $\Delta$ (or a closely related
self-adjoint operator), and let $\Gamma$ denote the underlying geometric or arithmetic input.
In a typical trace formula setting, one has a distributional trace functional
\[
\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h]
\]
defined on an admissible class of test functions $h:\R\to\C$.
In the Selberg framework, $\mathcal{L}_\Gamma$ decomposes into an identity term, a sum over conjugacy classes
(hyperbolic and elliptic contributions), and in the noncompact case an additional continuous-spectrum term.

In localized microlocal regimes, $\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h]$ is realized as the trace of a localized operator,
constructed using microlocal projectors and quantization of symbols.
The resulting expression depends on choices: a cutoff profile, a partition of unity in phase space, and a
quantization convention.  It is a fundamental analytic requirement that any meaningful invariant extracted from the
trace formula must be stable under these modifications.

The principal difficulty is that the identity sector introduces a zero-mode instability.
Even when $h$ is fixed, changing the cutoff profile alters the contribution of $h(0)$.
Similarly, changing quantization modifies lower-order terms in the kernel expansion.
While these discrepancies are expected to be controlled, a systematic canonicalization mechanism is rarely made
explicit.

The present work addresses this problem by introducing a normalization layer which separates the invariant content of
the trace from the auxiliary contributions and packages all remaining ambiguity into a single quantified remainder.

\subsection{Overview of the approach}
\label{subsec:intro.approach}

The analysis is based on three complementary components.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(i) Microlocal localization.}
We work with a microlocal decomposition of the trace, implemented through a family of projectors
$\{\Pi_j\}$ forming a partition of unity in phase space, and with a semiclassical quantization map $\Op$.
This yields a localized trace representation of the form
\[
\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h]
=
\sum_j \Tr\bigl(\Pi_j \, K_h \, \Pi_j\bigr) \;+\; \Err(h),
\]
where $K_h$ denotes the operator kernel associated with the test function $h$ and the error term is controlled by
the microlocal calculus.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(ii) Canonical normalization.}
To remove the instability at the identity sector, we fix a cutoff profile $\chi\in C_c^\infty(\R)$ with
$\chi\equiv 1$ near $0$ and define the normalization map
\[
\mathsf{N}_\chi(h) := h - h(0)\chi.
\]
The normalized trace functional is then defined by
\[
\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi] := \mathcal{L}_\Gamma[\mathsf{N}_\chi(h)].
\]
This subtraction isolates the geometric and dynamical content of the trace from the purely local contribution
encoded by $h(0)$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(iii) Error accounting and stability.}
The remaining dependence on auxiliary choices is quantified by an explicit error ledger.  The ledger consists of
separate components controlling changes of quantization, refinement of the microlocal partition, truncation tails,
and continuous-spectrum contributions.  The final output is a stability theorem showing that the normalized trace is
canonical modulo an explicit identity correction and a controlled remainder.

This approach is designed to be portable across several trace-formula contexts: compact and noncompact hyperbolic
surfaces, scattering settings, and more general semiclassical trace problems.

\subsection{Main results}
\label{subsec:intro.main-results}

The main results of this work can be summarized as follows.

\begin{theorem}[Canonical normalized trace functional]
\label{thm:intro.canonical-trace}
Let $\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h]$ denote the raw trace functional associated with the geometric input $\Gamma$.
Fix $\chi\in C_c^\infty(\R)$ with $\chi\equiv 1$ in a neighborhood of $0$.
Define
\[
\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi] := \mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h-h(0)\chi].
\]
Then $\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi]$ is independent of the cutoff profile $\chi$ modulo an explicit identity-sector
correction depending only on $h(0)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Cutoff change identity]
\label{thm:intro.cutoff-change}
Let $\chi_1,\chi_2\in C_c^\infty(\R)$ satisfy $\chi_j\equiv 1$ near $0$.
Then for any admissible test function $h$ one has the exact relation
\[
\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi_1]-\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi_2]
=
-h(0)\,\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[\chi_1-\chi_2].
\]
In particular, the cutoff dependence is confined to a deterministic term supported in the identity sector.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Normalization stability theorem]
\label{thm:intro.stability}
Let $\Op$ and $\Op'$ be two admissible quantization schemes and let $\{\Pi_j\}$ and $\{\widetilde{\Pi}_k\}$ be two
admissible microlocal covers.
Then for each admissible $h$ there exists a remainder term $\Err_{\mathrm{norm}}(h)$ such that
\[
\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi] - \mathcal{T}'_\Gamma[h;\chi']
=
\Delta_{\mathrm{id}}(h;\chi,\chi') + \Err_{\mathrm{norm}}(h),
\]
where $\Delta_{\mathrm{id}}(h;\chi,\chi')$ is an explicit identity correction and the remainder satisfies
\[
|\Err_{\mathrm{norm}}(h)|
\le
\mathcal{E}_\Gamma[h;\chi]+\mathcal{E}'_\Gamma[h;\chi'].
\]
Here $\mathcal{E}_\Gamma$ and $\mathcal{E}'_\Gamma$ are explicit error ledgers assembled from the microlocal calculus.
\end{theorem}

The above theorems provide a structural upgrade of localized trace methods: rather than working with a family of trace
identities depending on auxiliary choices, one obtains a canonical trace functional equipped with a quantitative
stability guarantee.

\subsection{Consequences and applications}
\label{subsec:intro.applications}

The normalization framework has two immediate analytic consequences.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(1) Local spectral asymptotics.}
The normalized trace functional may be used to derive local Weyl laws and microlocal spectral asymptotics in a form
stable under refinements of the localization scale.  In particular, one can isolate the main Weyl term and
control the remainder uniformly under admissible auxiliary changes.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{(2) Determinant-level trace-to-zeta bridges.}
Trace identities are naturally linked to zeta-regularized determinants and to explicit formulae in analytic number
theory.  The normalized trace functional provides a stable interface for passing from localized trace expansions to
determinant identities and positivity criteria, since all noncanonical contributions are isolated into explicit
identity corrections and a quantified error ledger.

This provides a conceptual path from microlocal trace formulae to zeta-type objects and to spectral constraints of the
Li--Weil type.

\subsection{Structure of the paper}
\label{subsec:intro.structure}

The paper is organized as follows.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:02-preliminaries}.}
We introduce the geometric and analytic framework, the relevant operator calculus, and the admissible test class.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chapters~\ref{chap:03-kernels}--\ref{chap:05-microlocal}.}
We construct the localized kernels and microlocal projectors and develop the remainder estimates needed for trace
localization.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:06-trace-formula}.}
We establish the localized trace formula and identify the decomposition into identity and nontrivial geometric terms.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:08-normalization}.}
We introduce the normalization layer and prove the stability theorem under admissible changes of auxiliary data.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:09-local-weyl}.}
We derive local Weyl asymptotics and formulate the results directly in terms of the canonical normalized trace and its
error ledger.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:10-trace-to-zeta}.}
We develop the trace-to-zeta interface and discuss determinant-level consequences and positivity mechanisms.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Chapter~\ref{chap:11-conclusion}.}
We summarize the results and indicate directions for further development.
