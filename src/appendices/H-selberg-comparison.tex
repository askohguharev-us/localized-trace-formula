\appendix
\chapter{Comparison with the classical Selberg trace formula}
\label{app:H.selberg}

\section{Purpose and positioning}
\label{sec:H.purpose}

The localized trace formalism developed in the main body of this monograph is designed to be stable under microlocal
choices and to produce quantitative remainder control in short spectral windows.  Nevertheless, the classical Selberg
trace formula remains the canonical reference point for hyperbolic surfaces, both as a conceptual model and as a
benchmark for leading terms and constant factors.

The purpose of this appendix is therefore threefold:
\begin{enumerate}
\item to record a precise dictionary between the normalized localized trace functional $\mathcal{T}_\Gamma$ and the
classical Selberg trace formula;
\item to verify that the identity, hyperbolic, elliptic, and parabolic components appearing in the localized framework
reduce to the standard Selberg expressions under global smoothing;
\item to isolate the precise location at which the localized framework introduces genuinely new structure, namely the
microlocal cutoff and the consolidated error ledger.
\end{enumerate}

Throughout this appendix we adhere to the Fourier and scattering normalizations fixed in
Appendix~\ref{app:G.fourier}.

\section{The classical Selberg trace formula: normalized statement}
\label{sec:H.classical}

Let $M=\Gamma\backslash\H$ be a finite-area hyperbolic surface with cusps.  Let $h:\R\to\C$ be an even function,
holomorphic in a strip $|\Im r|\le \sigma$ for some $\sigma>1/2$, and of sufficient decay so that all expressions below
converge absolutely.  Let $g$ denote its Fourier transform in the convention of
Appendix~\ref{app:G.fourier}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H.g.def}
g(t)
=
\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{\R} h(r)\,e^{-irt}\,dr.
\end{equation}

The Selberg trace formula asserts an identity of the form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H.selberg.master}
\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{disc}}[h]
+
\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{cont}}[h]
=
\mathcal{I}[h]
+
\mathcal{H}[h]
+
\mathcal{E}[h]
+
\mathcal{P}[h],
\end{equation}
where the left-hand side is the spectral contribution and the right-hand side decomposes into identity, hyperbolic,
elliptic, and parabolic terms.

We now record each component in a normalization consistent with the conventions of the present work.

\subsection{Discrete spectrum contribution}
\label{subsec:H.disc}

The discrete spectrum contribution is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H.disc}
\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{disc}}[h]
=
\sum_{j} h(r_j),
\end{equation}
where the discrete eigenvalues of $\Delta$ are written as
\[
\lambda_j=\frac14+r_j^2.
\]

\subsection{Continuous spectrum contribution}
\label{subsec:H.cont}

The continuous spectrum contribution is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H.cont}
\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{cont}}[h]
=
\frac{1}{4\pi}\sum_{\mathfrak{a}}
\int_{\R} h(r)\,\varphi_{\mathfrak{a}}(1/2+ir)\,dr,
\end{equation}
with $\varphi_{\mathfrak{a}}(s)$ denoting the logarithmic derivative of the scattering determinant contribution at
cusp $\mathfrak{a}$, in the convention fixed in Appendix~\ref{app:G.fourier}.

\subsection{Identity term}
\label{subsec:H.identity}

The identity term is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H.identity}
\mathcal{I}[h]
=
\frac{\vol(M)}{4\pi}
\int_{\R} h(r)\,r\tanh(\pi r)\,dr.
\end{equation}

This term is the global spectral density and is the origin of the Weyl asymptotic main term.

\subsection{Hyperbolic term}
\label{subsec:H.hyperbolic}

Let $\{\gamma\}$ range over primitive hyperbolic conjugacy classes in $\Gamma$, and let $\ell(\gamma)>0$ denote the
length of the corresponding primitive closed geodesic.
The hyperbolic contribution is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H.hyperbolic}
\mathcal{H}[h]
=
\sum_{\{\gamma\}}
\sum_{m\ge 1}
\frac{\ell(\gamma)}{2\sinh(m\ell(\gamma)/2)}\,g(m\ell(\gamma)).
\end{equation}

This is the standard length-spectrum encoding mechanism of the Selberg trace formula.

\subsection{Elliptic term}
\label{subsec:H.elliptic}

If $\Gamma$ contains elliptic elements, then for each elliptic conjugacy class $\{\gamma\}$ of order $q(\gamma)$ one
has an additional orbital integral contribution.  We denote the total elliptic term by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H.elliptic}
\mathcal{E}[h]
=
\sum_{\{\gamma\}_{\mathrm{ell}}}
\mathcal{E}_\gamma[h],
\end{equation}
where the explicit form of $\mathcal{E}_\gamma[h]$ is classical and may be found in standard references.
Since the principal focus of the present monograph is the cuspidal and hyperbolic geometry, we treat $\mathcal{E}[h]$
as a controlled finite correction term.

\subsection{Parabolic term}
\label{subsec:H.parabolic}

The parabolic term arises from the cusps and has a logarithmic character.
It can be written in the form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H.parabolic}
\mathcal{P}[h]
=
\frac{1}{4\pi}\int_{\R} h(r)\,\mathcal{P}_\Gamma(r)\,dr
+
\kappa_\Gamma\,g(0),
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{P}_\Gamma(r)$ is an explicit function depending on the scattering matrix and cusp widths, and
$\kappa_\Gamma$ is a cusp-dependent constant (typically involving $\log$ of cusp parameters and Euler's constant).

The exact expression is well known; see \cite[\S~2--3]{Hejhal1983} and \cite[Ch.~6]{Iwaniec2002}.
For the purposes of this appendix, the important point is that $\mathcal{P}[h]$ is explicitly computable and depends
only on cusp geometry.

\section{The normalized localized trace functional}
\label{sec:H.localized}

We recall the structural definition of the localized trace functional $\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h]$ and its normalized
version $\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h]$.

\subsection{Raw localized trace}
\label{subsec:H.raw}

The raw localized trace functional is constructed by inserting microlocal projectors and cutoffs into the wave kernel
representation.  In its most abstract form it is a distributional functional
\[
\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h]
=
\Tr\big(\Pi\,h(\sqrt{\Delta-1/4})\,\Pi\big),
\]
where $\Pi$ is a microlocal projector associated with a chosen partition of unity on phase space.
As discussed in Chapter~\ref{chap:05-microlocal} and Chapter~\ref{chap:07-main-results}, $\mathcal{L}_\Gamma$ is not
canonical: it depends on auxiliary choices, and the identity sector introduces a nontrivial instability at $t=0$.

\subsection{Normalization}
\label{subsec:H.norm}

Fix a reference cutoff $\chi\in C_c^\infty(\R)$, even, with $\chi\equiv 1$ near $0$.
Define the normalization operator
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H.norm.op}
\mathsf{N}_\chi(h)
=
h-h(0)\chi.
\end{equation}
Then the normalized trace functional is defined by
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H.norm.trace}
\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi]
:=
\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[\mathsf{N}_\chi(h)].
\end{equation}

The key structural theorem of Chapter~\ref{chap:08-normalization} asserts that $\mathcal{T}_\Gamma[h;\chi]$ is
canonical modulo an explicitly computable identity correction and a controlled remainder.

\section{Reduction to the classical Selberg trace formula}
\label{sec:H.reduction}

The comparison with Selberg is obtained by specializing the microlocal cutoff to the global identity operator.

\subsection{The global cutoff limit}
\label{subsec:H.global.limit}

Assume that the microlocal projector $\Pi$ is replaced by the identity operator (or, equivalently, by a partition of
unity which is exact and covers all of $T^\ast M$ without loss).  Then the localized trace reduces to the global trace:
\[
\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h]
=
\Tr\big(h(\sqrt{\Delta-1/4})\big).
\]
In this case the spectral side is precisely the left-hand side of the Selberg trace formula
\eqref{eq:H.selberg.master}.

Consequently, in the global limit the decomposition of $\mathcal{L}_\Gamma[h]$ into identity, hyperbolic, and cusp
contributions coincides with \eqref{eq:H.identity}--\eqref{eq:H.parabolic}.

\subsection{Identity term matching}
\label{subsec:H.identity.match}

In the localized formalism, the identity term arises from the diagonal singularity of the wave kernel near $t=0$.
In the global limit this singularity is known to produce the Plancherel density
\[
\frac{\vol(M)}{4\pi}\,r\tanh(\pi r).
\]
Hence the identity term computed in Appendix~\ref{app:A.identity} matches the Selberg identity term
\eqref{eq:H.identity}.

In particular, the leading Weyl constant $\vol(M)/(4\pi)$ is invariant under all admissible microlocal refinements.

\subsection{Hyperbolic term matching}
\label{subsec:H.hyperbolic.match}

The hyperbolic contributions in the localized trace formula arise from nontrivial conjugacy classes in $\Gamma$ and
are computed via stationary phase analysis of the wave kernel along the corresponding closed geodesics.
In the global limit, the orbital integral computation recovers exactly the Selberg hyperbolic term
\eqref{eq:H.hyperbolic}, with the amplitude factor
\[
\frac{\ell(\gamma)}{2\sinh(m\ell(\gamma)/2)}
\]
and the test transform $g(m\ell(\gamma))$.

This establishes that the length-spectrum encoding mechanism is preserved by the localized formalism.

\subsection{Parabolic term matching}
\label{subsec:H.parabolic.match}

In the cuspidal setting, the localized trace formula contains an explicit continuous-spectrum contribution and a
parabolic remainder ledger term.  In the global limit these combine to produce the classical Selberg parabolic term
\eqref{eq:H.parabolic}.

Thus the localized framework does not alter the analytic structure of the cusp contribution; it reorganizes it into a
form adapted to short-window localization and to microlocal cutoff stability.

\section{The principal structural difference: ledger versus formal remainder}
\label{sec:H.ledger.diff}

The classical Selberg trace formula is usually presented as an identity of distributions.
In many applications, the remainder analysis is performed ad hoc, depending on the test function family and the
regime of parameters.

By contrast, the present monograph isolates all noncanonical contributions into a consolidated error ledger
$\mathcal{E}_\Gamma[h]$, which has the following properties:

\begin{enumerate}
\item it is stable under admissible modifications of microlocal cutoffs and quantization schemes;
\item it is additive under the decomposition of the trace formula into identity/hyperbolic/parabolic pieces;
\item it is quantitatively estimable in the relevant short-window regime $\eta\ge \lambda^{-\theta}$.
\end{enumerate}

In this sense, the ledger should be interpreted as the localized analogue of the classical ``error term'', but with a
canonical meaning: it is not merely a bound, but a structural object encoding the non-invariant content of the trace
expansion.

\section{A compatibility lemma for short-window families}
\label{sec:H.compatibility.short}

We record the precise statement which ensures that the Selberg trace formula and the localized trace formula agree at
the level of short-window smoothing.

\begin{lemma}[Compatibility of Selberg and localized smoothing]
\label{lem:H.compatibility}
Let $\chi\in\mathcal{S}(\R)$ be even with $\chi(0)=1$, and let
\[
\chi_\eta(u)=\chi(u/\eta),
\qquad
h_{\lambda,\eta}(r)=\chi_\eta(r-\lambda).
\]
Then, in the global cutoff limit, the Selberg trace formula applied to $h_{\lambda,\eta}$ yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:H.compatibility.eq}
\Tr\big(h_{\lambda,\eta}(\sqrt{\Delta-1/4})\big)
=
\frac{\vol(M)}{2\pi}\,\lambda\eta
+
\mathcal{H}[h_{\lambda,\eta}]
+
\mathcal{P}[h_{\lambda,\eta}]
+
O(\lambda^{1-\delta}),
\end{equation}
where the implied constant depends only on finitely many Schwartz seminorms of $\chi$ and on $\Gamma$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
This is a direct consequence of the Selberg trace formula with the Fourier scaling rule
$\widehat{\chi_\eta}(t)=\eta\,\widehat{\chi}(\eta t)$ and the identity term asymptotic
\[
\int_{\R}\chi_\eta(r-\lambda)\,r\tanh(\pi r)\,dr
=
2\lambda\eta\int_{\R}\chi(u)\,du
+
O(\eta^2\lambda),
\]
together with the hyperbolic and parabolic bounds established in Chapters~\ref{chap:06-geometric} and
\ref{chap:07-main-results}.
\end{proof}

Lemma~\ref{lem:H.compatibility} provides the precise bridge by which the localized short-window Weyl law recovers the
classical Selberg asymptotic when microlocal cutoffs are removed.

\section{Consistency of constants and main terms}
\label{sec:H.constants}

The preceding discussion implies that the constants in the main theorems of this monograph are rigidly fixed by the
classical Selberg trace formula.
In particular:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the Weyl constant $\vol(M)/(4\pi)$ in the global counting function is invariant;
\item the local density constant $\vol(M)/(2\pi)$ in short windows is invariant;
\item the hyperbolic amplitude $\ell(\gamma)/(2\sinh(\ell(\gamma)/2))$ is invariant.
\end{enumerate}

Thus any admissible localized trace construction which modifies these constants would necessarily violate the Selberg
compatibility lemma and is therefore excluded by the normalization and stability framework.

\section{Concluding remarks}
\label{sec:H.conclusion}

The Selberg trace formula is the canonical global identity for hyperbolic surfaces.
The localized trace formalism developed in this monograph should be understood as a refinement of Selberg's framework,
not as a replacement: it preserves the spectral--geometric dictionary exactly, while introducing two new structural
layers essential for microlocal analysis in short windows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a normalization mechanism which removes the identity-sector instability and yields a canonical trace functional;
\item a consolidated ledger which packages all noncanonical contributions into a single quantified object.
\end{enumerate}

This appendix establishes that, after these upgrades, the localized trace formula reduces to the classical Selberg
trace formula in the global cutoff limit, and therefore inherits the full rigidity of Selberg's constants and orbital
integral structure.
